Iron is a chemical element; it has symbol Fe and atomic number 26. It is a metal that belongs to the first transition series and group 8 of the periodic table. It is, by mass, the most common element on Earth, right in front of oxygen, forming much of Earth's outer and inner core. It is the fourth most common element in the Earth's crust, being mainly deposited by meteorites in its metallic state.

Common Uses
Steel production, construction, automotive manufacturing, machinery, tools, bridges, ships, appliances, surgical instruments. Iron compounds used in pigments, catalysts, and magnetic storage. Essential trace element in biology — hemoglobin and myoglobin.

~3500 BCE
Earliest Known Use
Iron artifacts dating back to 3500 BCE have been found in Egypt. These early objects were made from meteoritic iron, identifiable by their high nickel content.
~1200 BCE
The Iron Age Begins
Widespread smelting of iron ore began around 1200 BCE in the Middle East and southeastern Europe, marking the start of the Iron Age and revolutionizing toolmaking, agriculture, and warfare.
1722
Scientific Study
René-Antoine Ferchault de Réaumur published a detailed study distinguishing iron from steel based on carbon content, laying groundwork for modern metallurgy.
1856
Bessemer Process
Henry Bessemer invented the first inexpensive industrial process for mass-producing steel from molten pig iron, transforming construction and engineering worldwide.
Etymology
The word "iron" derives from Old English īsern / īren. The chemical symbol Fe comes from the Latin ferrum, meaning "iron." The Latin word likely derives from earlier Semitic languages.
